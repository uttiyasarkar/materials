Comments from Prof. Sanjay Swain

1. P8: Why v is there? Isn’t supposed to be a normalized state of (phi 1 +/- phi 2)?

This expression of field is at the ground state where the energy is minimum after SSB. So, phi_1 is v and phi_2 is zero. We have changed the field notation to \eta and \xi to avoid confusion.

   

2. P11. Table 2.1: How do you experimentally measure the charge of quarks? For that matter even how to experimentally find our quark has spin=1/2 and has 3 colors?

There is no direct measurement of the fractional charge of the quarks, as they are never free and are always found in the bound states of hadrons (color confinement). To experimentally determine the fractional charge, color, and spin of the quarks, we look for processes like electron-positron annihilations. These annihilations can produce muon-antimuon pairs or quark-antiquark pairs which in turn produce hadrons. The hadronic events are evidence of quark production and the process itself describes the spin-1/2 nature of the quarks. The ratio of the number of hadron events to the number of muon events gives a measure of the number of “colors” of the quarks as well as the evidence of the fractional charge. If the ratio were 1, then there would have been no color charge and the ratio would have just accounted for the fractional charges. The evidence points to five quarks with fractional charges with three colors.

3. P11. Table 2.2: Is there a reason why the range of weak interaction (10^-16) is smaller than the one mentioned below (which is for strong interaction ~ 10 ^-13 as shown by you)?

The weak interaction is a short-range force because of the high mass of its messenger particles namely W and Z bosons. For a mass of ~100 GeV, one can calculate the range (r ~ 1/m) to be 10^-16 cm. On the other hand, the range of strong interaction depends on the distance scale where the linear confining term takes over the Coulomb term 1/r in the potential. This is typically the size of the nucleus of an atom which is about 10^-13 cm in radius.

   

4. P12: What is the meaning of unification? The three couplings should be exactly the same or they should be related or something else?

By unification, we mean that all three fundamental forces can be described by a common theory which includes different couplings and terms from all three forces. These unified theories have the same coupling strengths for all forces at some high energy and at lower energy scale the symmetry of the theory is broken leading to a difference in strengths.

        In the connection to the SM, we realize that the phrase “amazingly unifies” might be misconstrued and have thus changed it to “incorporates”.

   

5. P12: Well, it’s not a new problem. The electron is lighter than protons and needed some explanation why? It was raised by Dirac.

Thanks for this important remark. Yes, naturalness is a common problem in many theories. In the case of electrons, it is like a point particle, whereas the proton is a bound state of quarks and gluons. The observed mass of leptons (electrons etc.) and free quarks can be explained by the Yukawa coupling, however for protons and neutrons the observed masses are dominated by the strong interaction between quarks and gluons.

12. Do you have a diagram of how R-parity violation implies protons are unstable? No need to put in the thesis but for your information.

Proton decay is not possible within the SM due to the baryon number conservation. However, for R-parity violating SUSY scenarios, we can preserve the gauge symmetry of the SUSY Lagrangian and introduce terms that violate lepton and baryon numbers individually. This can lead to the decay of a proton into an electron and a pi_0. Below is a Feynman diagram for such a decay in the context of R-parity violating SUSY. Note that the couplings have to be very small in order to account for such decay, since the bound on proton decay lifetime is 10^32 years.

18. Fig. 3.2: "100% branching fraction" Do you think this is a reasonable assumption? If yes, based on what argument?

Yes, I think it is a reasonable assumption. We don't know if SUSY is there. So, the exact branching fractions of different processes are yet unknown. If there is any sign of SUSY first, we can look for discovery channels and determine the exact production cross-sections later. Our analysis strategy favors the production of Z bosons in the final state. So to maximize the production cross-section, we assumed a 100% branching fraction.

19. P28: what is the meaning of interaction length over 10? Is it radiation length? Are the interaction length and radiation lengths the same?

The sentence is a bit confusing, so we have changed it to “The presence of the HO effectively increases the thickness of the HCAL and provides over 10 times the interaction length as compared to no HO, thus improving the energy resolution.”

        No, (nuclear) interaction length and radiation length are not the same. Interaction length is the mean distance traversed by a hadron before undergoing an inelastic nuclear interaction and its energy reduces to 1/e times the initial value. Radiation length is a characteristic of a material, related to the 1-1/e th energy loss of high-energy electromagnetic particles interacting with it.

 

20. P28: but the same can be done with x and y coordinate, why use eta and phi?

We know that the collision is happening along the z-direction and the CM frame is always a preferable choice. In CMS, the interaction between the p-p is not a pointlike interaction, rather a parton-parton scattering leading to several vertices. If we had done calculations in x-y coordinates, we would run into trouble while reconstructing objects like hadronic jets, since the delta-theta (angle between two final state hadrons or particles) in the x-y plane is not a Lorentz invariant quantity.  Whereas, if we go to a (E,pt,eta,phi) plane, variables like delta-eta, delta-phi are Lorentz invariant in the transverse plane.

23. P42: Can there be other methods or ways to pick PV except for the highest pt^2 of tracks? Also, why pt^2, why not pt?

A number of other track variables other than pt^2, such as the number of tracks associated with the vertex, the sum of track weights, the largest track pt in the vertex, the scalar ∑pt_i, where i spans the three tracks with largest pt etc. can be used to pick PV (see CMS-AN-11-129). At present, the pt^2 ordering is mostly used. Here, by nominal PV, we mean the PV corresponding to the “signal”. Since the pt^2 is the scalar sum of all tracks coming from the vertex, the RMS sum is preferred over the pt sum.

24. Eq. (4.5): is it optimized? If yes what is optimized on?

No, it is not optimized. The mT requirement restricts the veto to situations consistent with a W boson decay where a W can give rise to tau which further gives rise to the isolated pion tracks (W boson mT<100 GeV for different pt^W).

25. P48: Is this true? I thought most of them decay close to PV?

Yes, the B-hadrons decay very close to PV with a typical decay flight-length of about 200 𝝻m. With the excellent impact parameter resolution the pixel detector has (ranging between 10 and 15 𝝻m), we are able to locate the secondary decay vertices inside a jet. These provide a tell-tale signature of the jet initiated by a b quark.

26. P49: This is a vector but transverse energy is scalar?

Well, being the magnitude of the missing transverse momentum vector, MET is a scalar quantity. To denote the missing transverse momentum (MET) vector, we have always used the vector symbol on p_T^miss.

27. P52: what is special about this value or why this small splitting?

This small splitting ensures enough boost to the final state Z boson. One can also choose models with higher splittings and perform the similar study. Our search is sensitive as long as the Z bosons are sufficiently boosted. We ensure it by fixing the splitting at 50 GeV. An alternative model where the splitting between gluino and NLSP is taken as half of the sum of gluino and LSP masses is taken and the effect on Z-boson reconstruction efficiency and the sensitivity of the search is tested. In such a scenario, the light quark jets can also become boosted after a certain threshold of the mass splitting becomes higher. More details can be found in the link1 and link2.

28. P52: if you would have taken a range 1-10 GeV, would it still not correspond to high Z momentum?

Yes, absolutely. One can go higher in LSP masses. We tested this for higher LSP masses and a similar scan is possible where LSP is larger, as long as the Zs’ are boosted. More details can be found in the link1 and link2.

In “T5ZZ” T5 stands for gluino pair production and ZZ indicates the decay of each gluino into Z bosons in the final state. A suitable reference [Phys. Rev. D 88 (2013) 052017] has been added to the thesis to clarify this nomenclature.

40. “ECAL L1 prefiring issue”: what is this exactly?

During 2017 data taking, the gradual timing shift of ECAL was not properly propagated to L1 trigger primitives in 2016 and 2017. As a result, by the end of 2017, a large fraction of L1 E-Gamma objects at eta >2.0 were associated with the previous bunch crossing. This led to “self veto” of events, as the L1 trigger forbade consecutive bunch crossings to fire. This is known as the ECAL L1 prefiring issue. More details can be found in Appendix A.1.1.

41. What is HEM?

During 2018 data taking, the Hadron Endcap Module (HEM) 15 and 16 were disabled. Thus the name HEM came.  More details can be found in Appendix A.1.2.
